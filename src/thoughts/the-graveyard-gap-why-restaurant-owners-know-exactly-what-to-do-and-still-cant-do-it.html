The Graveyard Gap: Why Restaurant Owners Know Exactly What to Do (And Still Can't Do It)

You know you should be posting more on Instagram. You know that guy who called about a private event three months ago never heard back from you. You know your website looks like it was built in 2019 — because it was.

You’ve known all of this for years.

The gap between knowing that stuff and actually doing it has a body count. I’ve started calling it the Graveyard Gap — where good ideas, good instincts, and good intentions go to die. Not because you’re lazy. You just don’t have the time, the team, or the money to make it all happen.

For most of the history of small business, that gap was permanent. The cost of doing the things you knew you should do was just too high.

That just changed.

The Illustrator at Little Jumbo

A few years ago I was bartending at Little Jumbo when I met an illustrator and animator. Talented guy. Could draw things that made you stop and stare.

I wanted some of what he had — not all of it, just enough to be dangerous. So I hired him for drawing lessons.

Last week I showed Mike at Four Top Restaurants the new Part & Parcel website. Watercolour illustrations across every page. Mike asked who did them.

I did. With an AI that can paint better than those lessons ever got me.

2014 to 2026. From paying an illustrator to teach me the basics, to being able to create an entire graphic novel without the budget of DC Comics or Disney.

That’s a collapse.

Not a gradual improvement. Not AI getting incrementally better. A structural collapse of the gap between what you can imagine and what you can execute.

2,500 Dots, One Uncomfortable Truth

My friend Reese sent me this chart last week and I can’t stop thinking about it.

Each dot represents 3.2 million people. 2,500 dots for all 8.1 billion humans on the planet. The color shows the most advanced AI interaction that person has ever had.

Almost all of it is grey. 84% of humans have never used AI. Ever. In any form.

The green strip at the bottom is 1.3 billion free chatbot users — people who’ve asked ChatGPT to write them a birthday message. The tiny yellow sliver is the 15–25 million who pay for it. And that single red dot you can barely see — the 2–5 million using AI for development and building.

We’re not in the middle of this shift. We’re at the very beginning. And the distance between that grey mass and that red dot is where the next decade of business advantage lives.

What Happens When the Constraint Disappears

“For the longest time a lot of small businesses would fail because they didn’t have the resources to have people to build all this stuff out, to answer the phones, to follow up — and now, for the first time ever, you can collapse the graveyard gap.”

The thing that held you back wasn’t your knowledge. You knew what to do. It was the bandwidth to do it — the budget for the team, the hours in the day, the technical skill to build the systems.

That constraint is dissolving. Not slowly. Not someday. Now.

Restaurant owners who couldn’t afford a marketing team are building follow-up systems, ad campaigns, and websites that would’ve cost $50,000 two years ago. They didn’t get smarter. The cost of doing the work just dropped through the floor.

“If you’re someone who’s had a niggling sense of the things you should be doing but aren’t, or you have great vision of how you want things to be but they maybe seemed too ambitious — there’s now a new paradigm you can enter.”

The Part Nobody Sees Coming

The restaurants getting busier from collapsing the Graveyard Gap aren’t replacing their staff. They’re hiring.

A busier restaurant needs more servers, more line cooks, more hosts. A roofing company with more jobs needs more roofers. The collapse doesn’t kill work — it creates it.

The constraint was never “too many employees.” It was “not enough business to justify more.”

Remove the constraint on execution, and you don’t get fewer jobs. You get more business. Which means more jobs.

The Orchestrator Shift

“You must be an orchestrator and accelerator of outcomes — if you’re looking at tasks or operating as a tool, you’re dead in the water.”

The old model: you do everything yourself, or you hire people to do it, or it doesn’t get done. The new model: you direct systems that execute at a scale you couldn’t have afforded before. Your job isn’t to do the work. It’s to know what work needs doing — and orchestrate the execution.

That list in your head? The one with all the things you know you should be doing?

It’s not a graveyard anymore. It’s a blueprint.

That’s a collapse.
